Question 1. The (anonymous) TrueCrypt authors have said, "Using TrueCrypt is not secure as it may contain unfixed security issues" (http://truecrypt.sourceforge.net/, 2014). Does the cryptanalysis provided above prove or disprove this assumption?

From the finding of the Audit Project, some of the main issues are relative to the operative system and its features. Some of the recommendations are updating independent parts of the OS. These recommendations reveal that the environment where TrueCrypt had to run greatly impacted its security performance. Others can argue that TrueCrypt could have adapted to use the OS more efficiently and securely. The difficult maintenance is not directly proportional to its security risk levels. However, it could impact it in the long term as all other activities are made slow with complex maintenance. As indicated in the studio, finding bugs also becomes a difficult task.

Finally, the study by Junestam & Guigo (2014) concludes with the absence of high-level severity vulnerabilities and, at the same time, shows that most vulnerabilities found are informational and low. These findings leave the definition of acceptable risks as the key to whether to adopt the solution. Models, metrics and methodologies are necessary to understand when the risks are acceptable, and analysis is needed to mitigate too high risks (Asnar & Zannone, 2008).

It is clear that TrueCrypt is not 100% secure, but in many cases, acceptable levels of security risks will determine if the solution is suitable or not. So it does not prove the assumption.

Question 2. Would you be prepared to recommend TrueCrypt to a friend as a secure storage environment? What caveats (if any) would you add?

Currently not. However, placing ourselves in the context of time and considering the definition of acceptable risk levels, and the availability of other solutions around that time, we would have recommended the solution with the following caveats.

If recoverability is critical and searchability very important, then another solution could meet the requirements in a better way.

The weak header volume key derivation described in the studio is the main cryptographic concern. The mounted volume's physical security will need to be a priority.

# Based on the algorithm presented at https://www.geeksforgeeks.org/how-to-encrypt-and-decrypt-strings-in-python/
import rsa

def encrypt_decrypt(the_string):

   public_key, private_key = rsa.newkeys(512)

   encrypted_string = rsa.encrypt(the_string.encode(), public_key)

   print("original string: ", the_string)
   print("encrypted string: ", encrypted_string)

   decrypted_string = rsa.decrypt(encrypted_string, private_key).decode()

   print("decrypted string: ", decrypted_string)

if __name__ == '__main__':
   the_string = str(input("Enter the string: "))
   encrypt_decrypt(the_string)
